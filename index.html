✦ THE PLANETS ✦
Mercury
Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest to the Sun. Despite its proximity to the Sun, it has extreme temperature variations, from scorching hot during the day to freezing cold at night. It has a rocky surface covered in craters, similar to Earth's Moon.
Diameter: 4,879 km Distance: 57.9M km Orbit: 88 days
Venus
Venus is the second planet from the Sun and the hottest planet in our solar system. Its thick, toxic atmosphere traps heat, creating a runaway greenhouse effect. Venus rotates backward compared to other planets, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east.
Diameter: 12,104 km Distance: 108.2M km Orbit: 225 days
🌍 Earth & 🌙 The Moon
Earth is our home and the only planet known to support life. It has liquid water on its surface, a protective atmosphere, and a magnetic field that shields us from solar radiation. The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite, influencing our tides, stabilizing our axial tilt, and has been a source of wonder for humanity. Together, they form a unique celestial pair in our solar system.
Earth Diameter: 12,742 km Moon Diameter: 3,474 km Distance: 149.6M km Earth Orbit: 365 days
Mars
Mars, known as the Red Planet, gets its color from iron oxide (rust) on its surface. It is home to Olympus Mons, the tallest volcano in the solar system. Scientists believe Mars once had liquid water and continues to explore it for signs of past life.
Diameter: 6,779 km Distance: 227.9M km Orbit: 687 days
Jupiter
Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system, more than twice as massive as all other planets combined. It is a gas giant with a famous Great Red Spot, a storm that has been raging for centuries. Jupiter has at least 79 moons, including the four large Galilean moons.
Diameter: 139,820 km Distance: 778.5M km Orbit: 11.9 years
Saturn
Saturn is famous for its stunning ring system, made primarily of ice particles and rocky debris. It is a gas giant with a low density — if there were a bathtub large enough, Saturn would float. Saturn has over 80 confirmed moons, with Titan being the largest.
Diameter: 116,460 km Distance: 1.43B km Orbit: 29.5 years
Uranus
Uranus is an ice giant that rotates on its side, with a 98-degree axial tilt. This unique orientation causes extreme seasons lasting about 42 years each. Its pale blue color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light.
Diameter: 50,724 km Distance: 2.87B km Orbit: 84 years
Neptune
Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun. It is known for its deep blue color and extremely fast winds, reaching over 2,100 km/h — the fastest in the solar system. Neptune has 14 known moons, with Triton being the largest.
Diameter: 49,244 km Distance: 4.5B km Orbit: 165 years
💫 Pluto (Dwarf Planet)
Pluto is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, a ring of bodies beyond Neptune. It was once considered the ninth planet of our solar system but was reclassified in 2006. Despite its small size, Pluto has a complex surface with mountains, valleys, and plains made of nitrogen ice. It has five known moons, with Charon being the largest.
Diameter: 2,377 km Distance: 5.91B km Orbit: 248 years Moons: 5 (Charon, Styx, Nix, Kerberos, Hydra)
